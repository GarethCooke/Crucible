Same queue API. Different tail by orders of magnitude. End-to-end
enqueue-to-dequeue, market-data thread to strategy thread.

## Setup

A market-data thread produces ticks; a strategy thread consumes them. We measure
the latency from the moment the producer stamps the item to the moment the
consumer has it in hand â€” the full journey across the queue. Three variants on
the same 16-byte `MarketTick`:

- **`lockfree-handrolled`** â€” ~50-line ring buffer, 1024 entries, power-of-2
  bitmask indexing. `std::atomic` head and tail, each on its own 64-byte cache
  line. `acquire`/`release` ordering only â€” no `seq_cst` in the hot path. Non-blocking
  `try_push`/`try_pop`; the harness spins on false.
- **`lockfree-boost`** â€” `boost::lockfree::spsc_queue<MarketTick, capacity<1024>>`,
  compile-time fixed size. Header-only. Exists as a sanity check on the hand-rolled
  implementation, not a competing entry.
- **`mutex-condvar`** â€” `std::queue` + `std::mutex` + `std::condition_variable`.
  Producer locks, pushes, calls `notify_one`. Consumer `wait`s on a predicate,
  pops, unlocks. The standard deployed pattern.

Producer pinned to core 4, consumer to core 5 â€” both on CCX1 of the Ryzen 7
3800X, sharing an L3 slice. Same-CCX only; cross-CCX is deferred.

The throughput gap between lock-free and mutex is smaller than the tail gap â€”
often within a small multiple. This is expected: the mutex fast path (lock
uncontested, no condvar wake-up) is cheap. The cost shows up only when the
condvar path fires. Saturated throughput masks this; real workloads with bursty
arrivals pay it constantly.

**The lock-free win is about determinism, not raw rate.**

## Why the tail collapses

The mutex cold path is a syscall. When `cv.wait` blocks, the kernel suspends the
consumer thread and reschedules it after the producer calls `notify_one`. The wake-up
latency includes:

1. A `futex` syscall from `notify_one` â€” kernel entry and return.
2. A scheduler decision: when does the consumer thread actually run?
3. Context restoration on the consumer core.
4. Cache cold effects if the consumer core has been executing other work.

None of that happens on the lock-free path. The consumer spins until it sees the
producer's `tail_` store (via `memory_order_acquire`), then loads the item. On Zen 2
with both threads on the same L3 slice, that cache-coherence round-trip is measured in
tens of nanoseconds. There is no kernel boundary.

The lock-free p50 and the mutex p50 may be close â€” the mutex fast path is fast when
the consumer is already spinning on the condvar. The p99 and p99.9 are where the
difference lives, because those represent the samples where the kernel path fired.

## Boost comparison

`boost::lockfree::spsc_queue` is a production-quality implementation with the same
structural properties as the hand-rolled version: power-of-2 capacity, `acquire`/`release`
atomics, cache-line-aware layout. If the hand-rolled queue tracks Boost within Â±10%
at both p50 and p99.9, that's a useful credibility check â€” it means the hand-rolled
implementation isn't leaving anything obvious on the table. Any material divergence
is discussed in the notes accompanying the run output.

## What this doesn't show

- **Cross-CCX**: producer and consumer on separate CCX slices. Cache-coherence traffic
  crosses the Infinity Fabric; latency increases. Deferred to a future topology post.
- **MPSC / MPMC**: single-producer single-consumer only. Multi-producer queues require
  different synchronisation strategies.
- **Saturated throughput only**: both threads loop as fast as possible. Bursty arrivals,
  offered-load testing, and back-pressure behaviour are out of scope.
- **Fixed item size**: 16-byte `MarketTick`. Queue behaviour is independent of payload
  contents but may differ with larger items (cache-line straddling, prefetch patterns).
- **Fixed queue depth**: 1024 entries. Shallower queues increase producer-stall frequency;
  deeper queues may not fit in L1d.
- **The spin-check-without-condvar mutex variant**: separating wake-up cost from lock cost
  is a different experiment and a different post.

## Takeaway

The lock-free SPSC ring buffer doesn't win on throughput â€” it wins on the shape of
the tail. For a latency-sensitive path where p99.9 determines whether the strategy
reacts in time, removing the kernel boundary is non-negotiable. The mutex fast path
is fast; its cold path involves a syscall and a scheduler decision, and those outliers
appear precisely when the market is moving fastest.
